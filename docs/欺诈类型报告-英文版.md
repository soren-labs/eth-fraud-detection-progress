# On-Chain Feature Extraction and Behavioral Logic for Ethereum Fraud Detection: A Comprehensive Literature Review

## Introduction to Ethereum Security and Fraud Taxonomy

The Ethereum blockchain has fundamentally rearchitected the infrastructure of decentralized finance (DeFi), transitioning the utility of distributed ledgers from simple peer-to-peer currency transfers to highly complex, Turing-complete execution environments. By enabling the deployment of smart contracts, Ethereum has fostered a multi-billion-dollar ecosystem comprising decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs), automated market makers (AMMs), lending protocols, and non-fungible token (NFT) marketplaces. However, the precise architectural innovations that empower this permissionless innovation—namely, pseudonymity, immutable code execution, and decentralized consensus—have simultaneously cultivated a highly fertile and anonymized operating theater for sophisticated cybercriminal activity. The immutability of smart contracts dictates that once malicious code is deployed or a fraudulent transaction is finalized on the ledger, the action cannot be unilaterally reversed or censored by a central authority. Consequently, the defense against financial exploitation on the Ethereum network relies almost entirely on proactive detection, continuous monitoring, and the advanced algorithmic classification of on-chain behavioral signatures.

The academic consensus surrounding blockchain security has increasingly pivoted toward the extraction and classification of on-chain transaction features to detect anomalous, high-risk patterns indicative of fraud. Traditional, rule-based cybersecurity frameworks and heuristic blacklists have proven wholly insufficient due to the evolving complexity of malicious smart contracts and the advanced obfuscation techniques employed by sophisticated threat actors, such as decentralized coin mixers and cross-chain bridges. As a result, contemporary academic research heavily leverages advanced statistical analysis, graph theory, and deep machine learning models to accurately classify malicious entities. An initial systematic PRISMA review of the literature spanning IEEE Xplore, the ACM Digital Library, and ScienceDirect yielded over 29,274 records, which, after rigorous filtering for methodology and focus, distilled down to 1,585 peer-reviewed studies dedicated to algorithmic fraud detection on decentralized networks. These studies highlight that while supervised learning models (such as Extreme Gradient Boosting, achieving up to 95% accuracy) and federated learning models (such as FedAvg) dominate the landscape, the true differentiator in detection efficacy lies in the quality of the feature extraction.

A rigorous synthesis of this expansive literature reveals a clear and highly structured taxonomy of cryptocurrency scams, primarily distinguished by their underlying economic mechanisms, the psychological vectors they employ against victims, and, most importantly, the immutable on-chain data footprints they leave behind in the public ledger. This comprehensive research report categorizes the five most prevalent types of fraud on the Ethereum network: Phishing Scams, Ponzi Schemes, Initial Coin Offering (ICO) Scams, Rug Pulls (including Honeypots), and Sybil Attacks. For each identified category, the report details the specific business scenarios, extracts the academically recognized on-chain data features utilized for detection, and exhaustively explains the underlying behavioral and economic logic that forces fraudsters to manifest these specific topological and temporal patterns. Furthermore, the report rigorously summarizes the methodologies of the core academic papers that pioneer these detection frameworks.

## Phishing Scams

### Business Scenario Description

Phishing on the Ethereum network represents one of the most widespread, lucrative, and rapidly evolving vectors for digital asset theft. In the traditional Web 2.0 paradigm, phishing attacks primarily target user credentials, passwords, or centralized banking information. In the Web3 ecosystem, phishing directly targets the cryptographic keys that govern asset ownership, or, increasingly, relies on deceiving users into signing malicious transaction approvals that grant attackers full control over their decentralized assets. The operational paradigm of Ethereum phishing has evolved significantly over the past several years. Initially, scammers utilized social engineering across social media platforms to lure victims into transferring Ether directly to Externally Owned Accounts (EOAs) controlled by the attacker.

However, as user awareness increased, threat actors shifted toward deploying highly sophisticated "phishing contracts". In this contemporary scenario, scammers create counterfeit decentralized applications (DApps) or mimic legitimate protocol interfaces. When a victim interacts with these fraudulent interfaces, they are tricked into executing state-changing transactions—specifically, invoking the ERC-20 `approve` function or the EIP-2612 `permit` function. These functions were legitimately designed to allow DeFi protocols to spend tokens on a user's behalf, but when granted to a phishing contract, they allow the contract to autonomously and maliciously drain the victim's entire wallet without requiring any further user interaction or authorization. Empirical studies have identified the catastrophic scale of this threat; a single large-scale phishing group was found to have deployed over 37,654 phishing contracts, responsible for launching hundreds of thousands of illicit transactions and resulting in over $190.7 million in unrecoverable financial losses. Given the lack of a fixed operational pattern and the continuous generation of new phishing addresses, traditional static detection methods prove highly ineffective, necessitating the use of dynamic, graph-based machine learning approaches.

### List of Core On-chain Features

To combat the proliferation of phishing contracts and EOA-based theft, academic detection frameworks have shifted away from simple explicit transactional features toward dynamic, implicit feature extraction using advanced transaction graph analysis. By treating Ethereum accounts as nodes and the transactions between them as directed, weighted edges, researchers model phishing detection as a complex node classification problem within a temporally evolving network space. The following features are the primary indicators of this fraud type:

The manifestation of a "star topology" within the transaction graph is one of the most heavily documented and academically recognized structural features of a phishing operation. The behavioral logic dictating this feature is deeply rooted in the economic constraints of the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM). Phishing operators frequently utilize thousands of distinct, disposable addresses (often referred to as spokes) to execute individual attacks, compartmentalizing their operations to evade rudimentary tracking and limit the collateral damage if a single address is blacklisted. However, this compartmentalization is strictly bound by economic reality: every transaction on Ethereum requires "gas" (transaction fees paid in Ether). To maintain operational cost-efficiency and streamline the laundering process, the attacker must rely on a central controlling address (the hub) to either systematically fund the disposable attack wallets with gas or to rapidly aggregate the stolen assets once the phishing payload is executed. This unavoidable economic dynamic results in a perfect or near-perfect star topology on the public ledger. It is characterized by an abnormally high out-degree (in the case of funding) or in-degree (in the case of asset aggregation) for the hub address, coupled with a severely limited transaction history for the spoke addresses beyond their single interaction with the hub. Researchers utilizing network embedding algorithms consistently identify this localized, highly centralized structural pattern as a primary, high-confidence indicator of coordinated illicit activity.

Beyond structural topology, the temporal footprint of a phishing address—specifically its remarkably short lifespan and the presence of high-frequency temporal bursts—is highly distinct from the behavioral footprint of a legitimate user or a standard decentralized application. The behavioral logic driving this feature is the concept of operational utility and threat mitigation. Once a phishing address is identified, reported by victims, and subsequently flagged by community block explorers (such as Etherscan) or integrated security protocols, its utility to the attacker drops to absolute zero. Consequently, fraudsters are forced to execute both their extraction and their subsequent money-laundering phases as rapidly as physically possible to prevent victims from attempting transaction front-running or utilizing asset-recovery whitehat services. Academic models classify this behavior through "implicit graph-based features" that mathematically measure temporal anomalies. The specific on-chain signature is an explosive burst of high-frequency, high-value transactions concentrated within an incredibly narrow timeframe (often a matter of minutes or hours), which is immediately and permanently followed by absolute dormancy. Unlike legitimate exchanges or active traders whose transactions are distributed relatively evenly over months or years, the phishing node exhibits a singular, violent spike in activity.

Furthermore, the integration of multi-order transaction information allows researchers to detect the cascading flow of funds. This involves tracking the path of the stolen cryptocurrency as it moves away from the initial phishing contract, often cascading through multiple intermediary wallets before finally arriving at a centralized exchange where it can be liquidated into fiat currency. The velocity and the specific algorithmic pathing of this multi-order flow are highly indicative of professional phishing operations attempting to obfuscate their digital trail.

### Academic References

The academic literature addressing Ethereum phishing relies heavily on Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs), temporally evolving neural networks, and advanced embedding techniques to isolate these topological and temporal features.

In the foundational paper "Phishing Scam Detection on Ethereum via Network Embedding," published in the *IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics: Systems*, the authors propose a novel network embedding algorithm named Trans2Vec. The researchers recognized that standard node embedding techniques failed to capture the nuances of financial transactions. Their methodology involved reconstructing the Ethereum transaction network based on crawled labeled phishing addresses and subsequently utilizing Trans2Vec to extract multi-order transaction information. By taking both the transaction amount and the exact timestamp into consideration alongside the topological connections, Trans2Vec generates highly accurate, low-dimensional vector representations of addresses, which are then passed to a one-class Support Vector Machine (SVM) to classify nodes as normal or phishing with exceptional accuracy.

Building upon static graph analysis, the paper "GrabPhisher: Phishing Scams Detection in Ethereum via Temporally Evolving GNNs," published in *IEEE Transactions on Services Computing*, addresses the dynamic nature of phishing. The researchers identified that standard GNNs suffer from catastrophic forgetting and fail to model the continuous, evolutionary pattern of accounts. GrabPhisher constructs the behavioral patterns of accounts as a diffusion network graph in continuous time, allowing the model to capture new transaction features based on existing transactions organically. Tested on large-scale, real-world Ethereum datasets, GrabPhisher proved highly capable of extracting dynamic temporal features—such as the rapid bursts of activity followed by dormancy—achieving a 95% Recall and an 88% F1-score, significantly outperforming previous static state-of-the-art methods.

Additionally, the paper "Phishing Tactics Are Evolving: An Empirical Study of Phishing Contracts on Ethereum" by He et al., accepted at *SIGMETRICS*, provides the first large-scale empirical analysis specifically targeting phishing contracts rather than EOAs. By collecting 37,654 phishing contracts deployed between late 2022 and early 2025, the authors identified suspicious functions directly from the bytecode and simulated transactions to uncover the modern behavioral traits of phishing groups, definitively mapping the shift toward contract-based approvals and revealing the massive financial impact of these evolving tactics.

## Smart Contract Ponzi Schemes

### Business Scenario Description

A Ponzi scheme is a classic, centuries-old financial fraud that promises abnormally high returns to investors with little or purportedly no risk. The core mechanism of the scheme relies entirely on generating returns for earlier investors utilizing the capital contributed by newer, subsequent investors, rather than from any legitimate profit-making enterprise or underlying asset appreciation. While illegal in the traditional offline world, the pseudonymous and decentralized nature of the Ethereum blockchain has allowed hustlers to adapt this ecosystem into what are known as "smart Ponzi contracts".

The psychological and behavioral lure of a smart Ponzi scheme is uniquely dangerous because it exploits the very ethos of blockchain technology: "code is law." Because the smart contract's code is public, immutable, and its execution is cryptographically enforced by the decentralized network, fraudsters cultivate a false sense of absolute security. Victims mistakenly believe that the transparency of the blockchain guarantees the legitimacy of the yield, operating under the assumption that because the contract cannot be altered or stopped by the creator, no one can arbitrarily steal their money. However, the fraudulent redistribution logic is hardcoded directly into the immutable bytecode. Academic researchers have formally defined the parameters of a smart Ponzi scheme through four strict, overlapping criteria: (R1) the contract redistributes money according to a specific, hardcoded logic; (R2) it receives funds strictly from incoming investors, generating no external revenue; (R3) investors only profit if a sufficient, mathematically determined number of subsequent investors join the scheme; and (R4) later investors face an exponentially higher, near-certain risk of total financial loss once the influx of new capital inevitably stalls.

### List of Core On-chain Features

The detection of smart Ponzi schemes is highly dependent on identifying the mathematical unsustainability of the contract's capital flows, fused with static analysis of its compiled bytecode. The core on-chain features utilized by researchers are heavily rooted in the statistical variance between incoming investments and outgoing redistributions, as well as the relationship between the contract and its original deployer.

The most critical indicators of a Ponzi scheme are found in the severe skew and imbalance of transaction inputs versus outputs over the contract's lifespan. The behavioral logic here is a strict mathematical necessity. By definition, a Ponzi scheme requires an exponentially growing base of new investors to satisfy the linear or fixed return promises made to the early adopters. Once the rate of new incoming capital decelerates, the scheme cannot honor its payouts and will rapidly collapse. Machine learning models heavily weight specific account history features to detect this dynamic, notably comparing the absolute count of incoming transactions (`Tx_in`) against outgoing transactions (`Tx_out`). More revealingly, models analyze the disparity between the number of distinct, unique addresses paying into the contract versus the number of distinct addresses being paid by it. Over its lifecycle, a Ponzi contract will exhibit a massive, top-heavy skew in these variables. Furthermore, statistical metrics such as the Gini coefficient, mean, standard deviation, and skewness of the transaction values are continuously evaluated, as they are highly indicative of the pyramid-shaped redistribution model inherent to the fraud.

Contract lifetime and the phenomenon of the terminal zero balance represent another primary set of features. Unlike legitimate decentralized exchanges, automated market makers, or algorithmic lending protocols that maintain deep, continuous liquidity pools over years of sustained operation, a Ponzi scheme is defined by an inherently terminal design. It exists only until the influx of new capital ceases. The "lifetime" feature—calculated as the temporal delta between the time of the first received transaction and the last executed transaction—is consistently and predictably short. More importantly, the terminal state of the contract's balance will almost always approach zero. As the initiators and the early adopters drain the pooled Ether through the automated execution of the contract's logic, latecomers are left holding an unrecoverable deficit, resulting in a drained contract.

Finally, initiator payment anomalies serve as a massive red flag in classification models. The behavioral logic is straightforward: the architect of a Ponzi scheme designs the system for personal enrichment. They must extract value from the contract without subjecting their own capital to the mathematical collapse risks faced by the victims. Using advanced eXplainable AI (XAI) frameworks such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), researchers have isolated the contract creator's interaction features as highly predictive. Specifically, detection models verify whether the contract initiator received outbound payments from the contract despite never having made a required initial inbound investment deposit. This asymmetrical capital flow, where the creator systematically skims profits or receives a disproportionate hardcoded "developer fee" from every incoming deposit without risking their own funds, is fundamentally contrary to legitimate DeFi investment vehicles and represents a definitive signature of a smart Ponzi scheme.

### Academic References

The methodology for detecting Ponzi schemes relies heavily on fusing the aforementioned transactional behavioral data with static bytecode analysis to achieve high precision and recall.

In the comprehensive study "Sharpening Ponzi Schemes Detection on Ethereum with Machine Learning" published by Galletta and Pinelli at the *DLT Workshop*, the authors constructed a reusable dataset of 4,422 real-world smart contracts (673 Ponzi and 3,749 non-Ponzi). The researchers recognized that purely code-based detection could be fooled by bytecode obfuscation, while purely transaction-based models historically suffered from low accuracy. By introducing a new set of refined behavioral features (such as the statistical skew of payments and initiator anomalies) and optimizing a Light Gradient Boosting Machine Classifier (LGBMC) using grid search cross-validation, they achieved superior classification quality. Crucially, they utilized SHAP values to interpret the exact contribution of each feature, validating that the transaction input/output skew and the initiator's asymmetrical withdrawal behavior were the primary drivers of the model's predictive power.

Addressing the inherent difficulty of imbalanced data in fraud detection, the paper "Ponzi scheme detection via oversampling-based Long Short-Term Memory for smart contracts" introduced the PSD-OL methodology. Real-world blockchain datasets possess vastly more legitimate contracts than fraudulent ones. PSD-OL tackles this by employing advanced oversampling techniques to populate the minority class of Ponzi scheme smart contracts. By taking both the contract account transactional features and the compiled contract code features into consideration, the researchers trained a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) deep learning network capable of capturing the sequential lifetime behavior of the contract, demonstrating high effectiveness on the well-known XBlock benchmark dataset.

Furthermore, the paper "SADPonzi: Detecting and Characterizing Ponzi Schemes in Ethereum Smart Contracts," published in the *Proceedings of the ACM on Measurement and Analysis of Computing Systems*, provides a highly rigorous methodology based on the symbolic execution of bytecode. The authors mathematically modeled the four defining criteria of a Ponzi scheme (R1-R4) and applied their SADPonzi tool to all 3.4 million smart contracts deployed by EOAs on Ethereum at the time. Their experimental results on benchmark datasets suggested that their bytecode-centric approach could achieve 100% precision and recall, identifying 835 previously unknown Ponzi contracts responsible for over 17 million US Dollars in victim losses, thus proving the immense value of combining static analysis with temporal transaction data.

## Initial Coin Offering (ICO) Scams

### Business Scenario Description

The Initial Coin Offering (ICO) boom of 2017 and 2018 marked one of the most volatile and speculative periods in the history of decentralized finance, functioning essentially as unregulated digital asset crowdfunding. In an ICO, a project team publishes a "whitepaper" detailing a proposed blockchain protocol, platform, or decentralized application, and subsequently mints a new custom token on the Ethereum network (typically an ERC-20 token). Investors send established cryptocurrencies—primarily Ether (ETH)—to the project's smart contract in exchange for the newly minted tokens, operating under the speculative assumption that the tokens will vastly appreciate in value once the project launches and the ecosystem matures.

However, the complete absence of regulatory oversight, mandated financial disclosures, and structural accountability created a massive vacuum that was immediately exploited by fraudsters. The business scenario of an ICO scam is remarkably straightforward: the project developers deliberately misrepresent the characteristics, technological feasibility, and business partnerships of their offering across listing websites, social media, and their whitepaper to drum up extreme hype. They meticulously screen for and target naïve or less sophisticated retail investors driven by the "fear of missing out" (FOMO). Once the fundraising period concludes and the developers have accumulated millions of dollars' worth of Ether, the project is abruptly abandoned. The developers never build the promised platform, the newly minted tokens become inherently worthless, and the stolen Ether is rapidly laundered through exchanges. In 2018 alone, scamming activity shifted from high-volume, low-yield phishing to sophisticated, multi-million dollar ICO exit scams, drastically altering the landscape of Ethereum crime. While the era of the traditional ICO has waned in favor of Initial DEX Offerings (IDOs), the core mechanics of misrepresentation and capital flight remain deeply relevant in modern token launches.

### List of Core On-chain Features

Detecting an ICO scam necessitates analyzing both the macro-level flow of capital into the project's genesis contracts and the subsequent off-ramping behavior of the developers. The on-chain features differ significantly from Ponzi schemes; while a Ponzi scheme relies on continuous redistribution to prolong the illusion, an ICO scam is fundamentally a capital accumulation mechanism followed by an abrupt, unidirectional exit.

The primary on-chain feature is a massive, unidirectional inflow of high-value assets (ETH) without corresponding operational outflows. The behavioral logic is tied to the lifecycle of legitimate software development. A legitimate decentralized project that raises $20 million in an ICO will subsequently exhibit a consistent, sustained pattern of outbound transactions over months and years as the team pays developers, covers auditing fees, funds marketing campaigns, and deploys capital to maintain the protocol's infrastructure. Conversely, an ICO scam exists solely to steal the initial raised capital. Therefore, the on-chain manifestation is a massive clustering of incoming transactions during the highly publicized token sale period, followed not by sustained operational spending, but by massive, bulk transfers of the accumulated wealth to a small handful of untraceable developer wallets, often within days of the ICO's conclusion.

Furthermore, academic research emphasizes the rapid fiat conversion pattern and exchange interaction features. The ultimate goal of the ICO scammer is to convert the ill-gotten cryptocurrency into fiat money (or stablecoins) to secure their real-world purchasing power before law enforcement or angry investors can coordinate a response. Fraudsters cannot achieve this on-chain; they must interact with centralized exchanges (CEXs) or sophisticated decentralized coin mixers (like Tornado Cash). Therefore, detection models utilize advanced heuristics, such as the peeling chain heuristic, to track how large sums of accumulated ETH are systematically and rapidly broken down over multiple transactions and routed toward known exchange deposit addresses. The high velocity and specific topological routing of these post-ICO funds—bypassing any interaction with actual smart contract development—serve as definitive features of a premeditated exit scam.

Finally, the anomaly of token distribution metrics is a critical feature. In a legitimate ICO, the newly minted ERC-20 tokens are distributed widely among the thousands of investors who contributed ETH. In many ICO scams, researchers have noted that the smart contracts are fundamentally flawed or purposefully manipulated, resulting in anomalous token distributions where the vast majority of the newly minted tokens remain under the control of the deployer, or are distributed to Sybil networks controlled by the scammer to artificially inflate perceived market cap before the inevitable dump.

### Academic References

Research into ICO scams frequently bridges the gap between pure computer science and behavioral economics, analyzing how fraudsters leverage on-chain mechanisms to exploit investor psychology.

In the paper "The Economics of Financial Scams: Evidence from Initial Coin Offerings" presented at the *American Economic Association*, Yu, Phua, Sang, and Wei investigate the market mechanics of 5,935 point-in-time ICO snapshots. Rather than purely focusing on bytecodes, the authors utilized on-chain analysis of Ethereum wallets to empirically prove that ICO issuers strategically screen for naïve investors by purposely misrepresenting the characteristics of their offerings. By analyzing the transaction histories and the overall sophistication of the wallets investing in these misrepresented ICOs, the study highlights how on-chain demographic data serves as a critical indicator of a scam's target vector and overall risk profile.

To trace the financial aftermath of these scams, the paper "Identifying Phishing Accounts on the Ethereum Blockchain" by Chen et al., published in the *Proceedings of the IJCAI*, delves into the necessary laundering phase of ICO and phishing scams. The authors emphasize that the ill-gotten cryptocurrencies from massive frauds (such as the infamous Bee Token ICO scam) must be cashed through exchanges. By recognizing that the transaction records of the public blockchain are publicly accessible, they developed methodologies targeting the entire blockchain ecosystem to track these rapid fiat conversion patterns, providing users with early warning systems against addresses exhibiting the high-velocity, unidirectional capital flight indicative of post-ICO laundering.

Chainalysis, a premier blockchain forensics firm frequently cited in academic literature, provided a longitudinal view of this phenomenon in their "Crypto Crime Report" series. Their empirical on-chain data collection from 2017 through 2018 demonstrated a paradigm shift: while the raw number of scams declined, a smaller group of innovative criminals executed complex ICO exit scams that generated millions of dollars, fundamentally altering the topological landscape of Ethereum crime from widespread, low-value phishing to concentrated, high-impact capital extraction.

## Rug Pulls and Honeypots

### Business Scenario Description

The advent of Automated Market Makers (AMMs) such as Uniswap, Sushiswap, and Curve radically transformed the Ethereum ecosystem by completely decentralizing the concept of asset exchange. In a traditional financial market or a centralized cryptocurrency exchange (CEX), a central authority acts as a gatekeeper, auditing projects and approving assets for trading. In an AMM, the environment is entirely permissionless; any user can create a liquidity pool pairing a newly minted, fundamentally worthless token with a highly liquid and valuable asset like Ether (ETH) or a stablecoin. The pricing of assets within these pools is not determined by an order book, but rather by deterministic algorithms, most notably the constant product formula ($x \cdot y = k$), making them highly predictable and consequently, highly susceptible to manipulation by the pool's creator.

This unregulated, permissionless environment birthed the "Rug Pull"—a devastatingly effective scam where developers artificially inflate the perceived value and social media hype of a newly listed token, attract massive amounts of victim liquidity into the AMM pool, and subsequently execute a maneuver that reduces the token's value to zero. Academic classifications generally divide rug pulls into three distinct categories: *Simple Rug Pulls*, where the developer uses their administrative privileges to directly remove all the valuable ETH reserves from the liquidity pool; *Sell Rug Pulls*, where the developer dumps massive, pre-mined token reserves onto the market, crashing the price and extracting the ETH through legitimate trades; and *Trapdoor Rug Pulls (Honeypots)*, where malicious smart contracts are coded with hidden state variables that allow victims to buy the token but permanently disable their ability to sell it back to the pool. In 2021 alone, rug pulls accounted for 37% of all cryptocurrency scam revenue, representing billions in losses.

### List of Core On-chain Features

To move beyond retrospective forensic analysis and detect an impending rug pull before the malicious extraction maneuver actually occurs, academic researchers have pioneered the use of advanced economic heuristics and static code vulnerability features.

The application of the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) to token distributions is the most critical macroeconomic feature utilized for zero-day rug pull prediction. The behavioral logic of the HHI in this context is paramount. To successfully execute a sell rug pull, or to maintain monopolistic control over the liquidity pool's pricing curve, the malicious developer must hold a massive, heavily concentrated share of the token's total supply. If the supply is highly decentralized among tens of thousands of independent, organic holders, the developer lacks the leverage to unilaterally crash the market. Academic frameworks compute the HHI over the account balances of the token to quantify this concentration. The HHI is calculated by squaring the market share of each holding address and summing the results ($HHI = \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i^2$). A severely elevated HHI—indicating that the top handful of wallets control a massive percentage of the supply—acts as a primary, quantifiable heuristic warning of an imminent, high-risk rug pull scenario.

Coupled with the HHI is the Clustering Transaction Coefficient, a metric derived from transaction graph analysis. The behavioral logic dictating this feature revolves around the necessity of manufacturing fake volume. Legitimate tokens experience organic token propagation; as adoption grows naturally, users trade with a diverse array of decentralized exchanges, smart contracts, and independent users. Conversely, fraudulent tokens require the illusion of high trading volume and liquidity to trend on data aggregators (like CoinGecko or DEXTools) to attract retail victims. Scammers achieve this through "wash trading"—routing the same tokens back and forth between a tight, internally controlled cluster of Sybil addresses. The clustering transaction coefficient mathematically measures the degree to which nodes in the token's network cluster together, specifically evaluating whether the neighbors of a given node are also connected to each other. A manipulated network exhibits an anomalous, highly incestuous clustering topology that sharply contrasts with the broader, less interconnected graphs of legitimate tokens.

Furthermore, malicious code risks and lock contract misdirection serve as critical static features. To artificially manufacture trust and counter the inherent skepticism of DeFi investors, scammers often utilize third-party "liquidity lockers" (e.g., Unicrypt) to publicly prove on the blockchain that their liquidity tokens are cryptographically locked and cannot be removed. However, the behavioral logic of a trapdoor/honeypot circumvents this entirely. The scammers embed hidden trapdoors in the ERC-20 token contract itself, allowing them to arbitrarily mint new tokens to dilute the pool, or they insert `require` statements that disable the `transfer` function for all addresses except their own whitelisted wallets. Static analysis of the token's bytecode to detect these specific restriction flags is a core feature that must be fused with the HHI and clustering heuristics to accurately predict honeypot variations of the rug pull.

### Academic References

The academic methodology surrounding rug pull detection has made significant strides in shifting from post-mortem analysis to real-time predictive modeling.

The foundational paper in this domain is "Do not rug on me: Zero-dimensional Scam Detection" by Mazorra, Adan, and Daza. The authors recognized that existing algorithms were only valuable for detecting scams after the victims had lost their funds. To achieve zero-day predictive detection, they collected a massive dataset encompassing all transactions related to the Uniswap V2 exchange up to late 2021, expanding the analysis to 27,588 tokens. By engineering new relevant features—specifically the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index to measure supply concentration and the clustering transaction coefficient to measure token propagation heuristics—they trained machine-learning-based algorithms (including XGBoost and FT-Transformers) to evaluate the state of the liquidity pool. Their best model achieved an astonishing accuracy of 0.9936, a recall of 0.9540, and a precision of 0.9838 in distinguishing non-malicious tokens from scams strictly *prior* to the malicious maneuver occurring.

Expanding upon this, the paper "RPHunter: Unveiling Rug Pull Schemes in Crypto Token via Code-and-Transaction Fusion Analysis" addresses the highly complex intersection of trapdoors and tokenomics. The authors noted that pure transactional analysis struggles to differentiate between a heavily concentrated legitimate project and a honeypot. RPHunter integrates complementary information from malicious code risk (static bytecode analysis) and suspicious transaction behavior (HHI and clustering). By utilizing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for graph feature learning, the RPHunter framework effectively detects complex rug pull schemes, achieving an F1 score of 94.5% on their datasets and successfully identifying 4,801 newly created rug pull tokens on the Ethereum Mainnet with a 90.7% real-world precision.

## Sybil Attacks

### Business Scenario Description

A Sybil attack, originally conceptualized in the context of peer-to-peer network routing, has evolved into a massive financial threat in the context of Ethereum DeFi and protocol governance. An on-chain Sybil attack occurs when a single adversarial actor systematically generates a multitude of pseudonymous identities (wallets or accounts) to subvert a decentralized system that assumes and requires proportional human representation. While early blockchain Sybil attacks targeted the fundamental consensus mechanisms of the network itself (such as the devastating 51% attacks levied against the Ethereum Classic chain in 2020) , modern on-chain Sybil behavior predominantly targets the application layer.

Specifically, Sybil rings are deployed to extract value from token airdrop distributions, exploit liquidity mining incentive programs, and maliciously hijack DAO governance voting. Because decentralized finance increasingly relies on open, permissionless systems designed to reward active participation, the risk is existential. An attacker operating a botnet of 10,000 scripted wallets can effortlessly manipulate a protocol's growth metrics, drain airdrop rewards intended for legitimate early adopters, and outvote the organic community in governance proposals. Distinguishing a highly sophisticated bot network from a high-volume, organic human user is exceptionally difficult, as each malicious wallet is painstakingly designed to look perfectly plausible when viewed in isolation. Detection requires analyzing the macro-level behavior of the ecosystem to find the subtle, connective tissue linking the thousands of seemingly independent accounts.

### List of Core On-chain Features

To successfully identify a Sybil ring, detection models must analyze the entire lifecycle of the wallets involved—from their initial funding to their coordinated interaction with the target protocol, and finally to their extraction phase.

The "First Gas Acquisition" correlation and the presence of a "1-to-N" funding pattern form the most foundational structural heuristic for Sybil detection. The behavioral logic behind this feature is rooted in the fundamental architecture of the Ethereum Virtual Machine: the EVM strictly prohibits transaction execution without the payment of base computational fees (gas). Therefore, an attacker looking to operate 10,000 Sybil wallets cannot simply generate the cryptographic keypairs offline and immediately interact with a protocol; they must explicitly fund every single virgin wallet with a fractional amount of ETH to cover future transaction fees. Because funding 10,000 wallets individually through manual transfers or from separate, KYC-compliant centralized exchanges is logistically impossible and would trigger massive anti-money laundering flags, the attacker is forced to use an automated on-chain distribution script from a central funding source. This logistical bottleneck creates a highly detectable on-chain footprint. Subgraph-based models trace the origin of the very first inbound transfer to a wallet. The presence of a "1-to-N" distribution—where a single address executes rapid, minimal value transfers (often referred to as faucet-pattern behavior) to thousands of new wallets solely to cover the threshold of necessary transaction fees—stands out as entirely non-organic behavior and strongly signals the birth of a Sybil ring.

Following the funding phase, temporal synchronization and activity clustering become the primary indicators during the attack phase. Managing a massive network of wallets requires programmatic automation. The attacker's scripts execute interactions with the target DApp—such as claiming an airdrop or casting a governance vote—in a highly regimented, sequential, or simultaneous manner to maximize efficiency before the protocol developers detect the anomaly. Academic feature extraction heavily weighs temporal features, specifically extracting the timestamp of the first transaction, the precise timing of the airdrop participation, and the timestamp of the last transaction. Sybil addresses exhibit extreme temporal clustering; they engage in nearly identical event operation lifecycles. Machine learning models analyzing these features routinely demonstrate that the standard deviation of action timestamps across a suspected Sybil cluster is infinitesimally small compared to the randomized, chaotic action times of organic human communities scattered across global time zones.

Finally, the topological extraction of mesh versus star/tree subgraph structures provides the definitive proof of coordination. After the incentivized action is complete and the reward is successfully claimed across the 10,000 wallets, the attacker must consolidate the highly fragmented assets into a central repository to liquidate them, once again attempting to minimize the gas fees associated with multiple transfers. This consolidation phase directly mirrors the initial funding phase. Researchers construct multi-layer deep transaction subgraphs for each address to calculate degree features (in-degree and out-degree) and neighbor interactions. In legitimate protocol interactions, the flow of funds often resembles a complex, decentralized partial mesh topology, as thousands of independent users route funds to various personal wallets, exchanges, and diverse DeFi protocols. In a Sybil attack, the extraction results in a rigid, perfect star or tree topology. The highly synchronized, unidirectional flow of assets from thousands of spokes back to a single hub provides incontrovertible proof of automated control and eliminates the possibility of organic, independent action.

### Academic References

Recent literature highlights the efficacy of Subgraph-based methodologies combined with advanced gradient boosting frameworks to achieve high-fidelity Sybil detection across massive datasets.

In the highly influential paper "Detecting Sybil Addresses in Blockchain Airdrops: A Subgraph-based Feature Propagation and Fusion Approach" published by Liu, Huang, Fan, Wu, and Tang, the researchers tackle the massive scale of airdrop farming. The authors proposed a novel identification method based on subgraph feature extraction utilizing LightGBM. The core of their methodology involved constructing a two-layer deep transaction subgraph for every address in the ecosystem and extracting key event operation features strictly according to the lifecycle of a Sybil address (specifically targeting the time of the first transaction, the "first gas acquisition" timestamp, airdrop participation time, and last transaction). By propagating and fusing these temporal features with amount features and network structure degree calculations, their model was able to deeply comprehend the topological footprint of the network. The researchers conducted their experiments on a massive dataset containing 193,701 unique addresses, of which 23,240 were confirmed Sybil addresses. Their subgraph-based LightGBM model vastly outperformed existing approaches, yielding precision, recall, F1 score, and AUC metrics uniformly exceeding 0.90, demonstrating that structural and temporal consistency are the ultimate betrayers of automated botnets.

Additionally, applied research by institutions such as Gitcoin's Fraud Detection & Defense (FDD) workstream and the Cred Protocol outlines the transition of these academic features into real-time production APIs. By computing Sybil scores based on the entropy of transaction times, funding source heuristics, and verification of identity graphs, these frameworks validate the academic consensus: evaluating an address in isolation is futile, but evaluating the temporal and structural relationships of its subgraph provides definitive, actionable risk levels for DAO governance and DeFi lending.

## Conclusion and Future Trajectories

The evolution of fraud on the Ethereum blockchain represents a continuous, highly sophisticated arms race between adversarial ingenuity and cryptographic security. Traditional, rule-based cybersecurity frameworks and static heuristic blacklists are rapidly becoming obsolete in the face of this threat. Scammers possess the capability to continuously adapt their methodologies to evade rudimentary detection—transitioning from simple social-engineering EOA phishing to deploying complex, automated phishing contracts; shifting from the overt, highly publicized ICO exit scams of 2017 to the nuanced, mathematically manipulative Automated Market Maker (AMM) rug pulls of the modern DeFi era; and advancing from brute-force 51% network consensus attacks to highly coordinated, multi-layered Sybil rings designed to systematically siphon value from application-layer tokenomics.

However, the findings rigorously synthesized throughout this report demonstrate a critical, underlying vulnerability inherent to all forms of on-chain fraud: the immutable economic and architectural constraints of the Ethereum Virtual Machine itself. Regardless of the cryptographic obfuscation employed or the novelty of the decentralized application being exploited, malicious actors are strictly, unavoidably bound by the necessity to pay computational gas fees, maintain and manage deep liquidity, and coordinate complex multi-step interactions across the public network.

These rigid constraints reliably and consistently force fraudsters into manifesting highly specific, mathematically quantifiable behavioral patterns on the ledger. The absolute necessity to minimize transaction costs inevitably generates highly localized star-topology networks; the logistical necessity to orchestrate and fund massive botnets inevitably creates correlated "first gas acquisition" timestamps and highly synchronized action entropies; and the necessity to manipulate decentralized market prices inevitably results in extreme Herfindahl-Hirschman Index concentrations. By transitioning away from analyzing static variables and instead leveraging Graph Neural Networks, Temporally Evolving Neural Embeddings, and Ensemble Machine Learning algorithms, contemporary academic frameworks have proven highly proficient at isolating these structural and temporal anomalies. Future research trajectories indicate that the path forward lies in integrating these sophisticated on-chain topological features with cross-chain data fusion, enabling zero-day, real-time threat detection that can secure the continued maturation of the decentralized financial ecosystem.
